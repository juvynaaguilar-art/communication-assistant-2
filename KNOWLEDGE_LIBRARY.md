Core Concepts of Inclusive Education
Defining Inclusion
●
Belonging vs. Placement: Inclusion is not an instructional strategy or a simple placement option; it is fundamentally about belonging to a
community, a group of friends, or a school.
●
Beyond "Being There": Simply being in a room with others does not constitute inclusion if the individual does not feel like a true member of
the group.
The Role of Instruction
●
●
●
Intentionality: Inclusion does not happen naturally. It must be intentional, planned, and data-based.
Teaching Social Skills: Children with and without disabilities will not naturally interact if left alone. Educators must actively teach interaction,
friendliness, and helpfulness.
Instructional Failure: Student failure is often instructional failure. If a student isn't making progress, it means the instructional strategy
needs to change, not that the student is incapable.
Core Outcomes of Inclusion
1. Membership: How a child interacts with the group and what accommodations are made to help them participate meaningfully.
2. Relationships: The range of one-to-one interactions, including being a peer, helping others, receiving help, and navigating conflicts.
3. Skills: While academic and functional skills are vital, they are insufficient if the individual cannot use them within a group or relationship.
The "Why" of Inclusion
●
Diversity in Action: Inclusion is the celebration of diversity put into action.
●
Universal Benefit: When smart instructional practices are used to facilitate inclusion, everyone benefits—both those with and without
disabilities are supported and challenged.
Teacher-Paraprofessional Collaboration & Systems
The Preparedness Gap
●
The "Figure It Out" Trap: Most teacher preparation programs provide little to no training on how to supervise or manage the work of
paraprofessionals. As a result, teams often rely on the specific "culture" of their first school to learn how to collaborate, which leads to
inconsistent practices across different classrooms.
●
Imbalance of Power: While the technical definition of the relationship is "Teacher Supervises, Paraprofessional Supports,
" viewing this as a
power struggle is counterproductive. Successful teams shift from a "boss/assistant" mindset to a synchronized partnership.
Daily Communication Protocols
●
The 5-Minute Bookend: Begin and end each day with brief, informal check-ins. This ensures both partners are moving in the same direction
and clarifies roles before the day becomes "hectic.
"
●
Specific Role Clarity: Don't leave anyone guessing. Clearly define who handles specific tasks such as behavior management, instruction,
or transitions. Written guidance, like a daily responsibility list, acts as a "mini-command center" to keep the team aligned.
●
Plain Language: Avoid "teacher-prep" jargon. Using clear, plain language ensures that all staff—regardless of their educational
background—understand the instructional goals.
The Feedback Loop
●
Modeling over Explaining: When a strategy is new, teachers should model it side-by-side with the paraprofessional. Demonstrating the skill
builds significantly more confidence than a verbal explanation.
●
●
Invited Honesty: Paraprofessionals often hesitate to share insights unless explicitly invited. Use open-ended questions like,
"How did that
strategy feel for you?"
or
"What did you notice right before the student became frustrated?"
The "Expertise" Exchange: Recognize that paraprofessionals often have more "intimate" knowledge of a student's subtle behavioral
patterns (like jaw clenching or humming) because they are often the ones providing the direct, hands-on support.
Impact on Student Outcomes
●
Unified Front: When communication is inconsistent, it creates "threats and distractions" for neurodivergent students. High-quality adult
relationships are the single most important predictor of classroom success.
●
Data-Driven Partnerships: Both partners should be involved in data collection. Because paraprofessionals see students in various settings
(recess, lunch, specials), their qualitative observations are essential for accurate IEP progress monitoring.
Systemic Barriers and Cognitive Load
The Training Gap and "Figuring It Out"
●
●
●
Lack of Formal Preparation: Most teacher education programs (General Ed, Special Ed, and ESL) provide little to no formal coursework on
how to supervise, organize, or manage the work of paraprofessionals.
The Environment Trap: Because of this lack of training, staff members usually "figure it out" once they reach the classroom. This means
their collaboration style is often dictated by the specific culture of their first school, leading to inconsistent practices across different districts
and buildings.
Power Dynamics vs. Partnership: While the technical definition is "Teacher Supervises, Paraprofessional Supports,
" viewing this through a
lens of power is the wrong perspective. The focus should be on a synchronized working relationship rather than a hierarchy.
Adult Communication as a Student Barrier
●
●
●
Inconsistent Cues: When two adults in a room provide different instructions or have different expectations, it creates "Conflicting
Instructional Cues.
" For a student, this is not just a logistical error; it is a significant barrier to learning.
Frequent Interruptions: When staff members interrupt one another during planning or instruction to handle logistics (e.g.,
"Did the bus
come?"), it breaks the flow of support and prevents the team from reaching full alignment.
Cognitive Load and Stress: For neurodivergent students, adult misalignment acts as a "threat" to the brain. When students have to spend
mental energy decoding which adult to listen to or what a vague instruction means, their cognitive load increases, leading to classroom
stress and decreased self-efficacy.
The Shift to Synchronized Communication
●
●
Proactive over Reactive: Teams must move away from a reactive "figure it out" approach toward a proactive, intentional design.
The Value of Belonging: Synchronized communication doesn't just help the adults; it fosters a sense of belonging for the student. When the
adults are a unified front, the student feels safe, supported, and ready to engage with the content.
●
Professional Self-Efficacy: Transitioning to a synchronized team approach increases the professional confidence of both the Education
Specialist and the Paraprofessional, allowing them to feel more effective in supporting diverse learners.

The Core Framework
●
●

"You seemed disengaged.
"

"I noticed you were looking at your phone while I was explaining the student's transition plan.

○
Example:
"The impact was that I felt unaligned on the safety protocol, which made me feel anxious when the transition began.

●
